# ABI Salvage Categorisation — Decision Reference

Purpose: determine the ABI salvage category (A / B / S / N) for a motor vehicle, justify it, and identify the regulatory notification obligations. All motorised vehicle salvage in scope must be placed in exactly one of the four categories. The AQP applies good engineering practice and safety considerations and is responsible for the final category.

Scope: "motor vehicle" per s.185(1) Road Traffic Act 1988 / s.136(1) Road Traffic Regulation Act 1984 — a mechanically propelled vehicle intended or adapted for use on roads. Purpose of the CoP: protect the public, detect/deter insurance fraud and other crime, and ensure transparency of vehicle history.

---

## The four categories

| Cat | Name | Repairable? | One-line test |
|-----|------|-------------|---------------|
| **A** | Scrap / Recycle | No | Unsuitable / beyond repair AND no reusable parts. Crush in entirety after depollution + removal of recyclable components. |
| **B** | Break | No | Structural frame/chassis beyond repair, BUT suitable parts can be reused. Dismantle for parts; crush the structure. |
| **S** | Repairable Structural | Yes | Repairable, but damage affects the structural frame/chassis (incl. structural HV battery). |
| **N** | Repairable Non-structural | Yes | Repairable, with NO damage to structural framework/chassis. Cosmetic/bolt-on. May still need safety-critical replacements. |

Crisp distinctions:
- **A vs B**: both are non-repairable and both get the structure crushed. The difference is reuse — A has no reusable parts (whole vehicle crushed); B has reusable parts (vehicle broken for spares, then the VIN-bearing structure crushed). If a Category-A vehicle is later dismantled for reuse, Category B criteria apply.
- **B vs S**: both involve structural-frame damage. B = the structure cannot be repaired (break it). S = the structure is repairable but the owner chose not to repair (sold as repairable salvage). Where no original structural components are available, or repair would rely on second-hand structural framework/elements, the vehicle is **never** repairable → Category B (exception: vehicles ≥7.5 t, §14.2).
- **S vs N**: both repairable. S = any structural element requires repair, realignment to original dimensions, or replacement. N = no structural damage; only cosmetic/outer/bolt-on damage (but safety-critical items may still need replacing). Cosmetic repair to a structural element with no realignment required does NOT make it S.

A "catastrophically damaged vehicle" cannot be safely repaired to accepted industry standards without a bodyshell, or where manufacturer/researched repair methods or service parts are unavailable, or it has prior substandard repair / excessive corrosion. These are taken out of the repair chain (→ A or B).

## Categorisation decision framework (the four questions)

Work the flowchart top-down in order **1, 2, 3** (non-repairable branch) **or 1, 2, 4** (repairable branch).

1. **Q1 — Is the damage sufficiently severe to warrant application of this CoP?**
   - No / minimal damage only → outside the CoP (no category needed).
   - **Any structural damage → always categorise under the CoP** (cannot fall outside).

2. **Q2 — Is the salvage vehicle suitable for repair?** (the most important decision; directly linked to road-user safety)
   - Excluding water/fire damage, can it be safely repaired to accepted industry standards using manufacturer / recognised researched repair methods?
   - Remove from any theft chain (e.g. a stripped vehicle needing unobtainable/expensive parts should not be called repairable, or a similar car may be stolen and broken for parts).
   - No original structural components available, or repair would use second-hand structural framework/elements → **never repairable → Category B** (exception §14.2, ≥7.5 t).
   - **NOT repairable → go to Q3. Repairable → go to Q4.**

3. **Q3 — Should any parts be reused?** (non-repairable branch)
   - **No reusable parts → Category A.**
   - **Reusable parts → Category B.**
   - Reasons an insurer may require whole-vehicle crush (→ A) even with apparently usable parts: fire/chemical/heat contamination (§13.2); water/biological contamination (§13.1); H&S contamination (biological, chemical, drugs); police request; ethical-grounds request. Removal of any components must use correct practices and PPE.

4. **Q4 — Has the vehicle sustained structural damage (including a structural HV battery)?** (repairable branch)
   - **Yes (structural) → Category S.**
   - **No (non-structural only) → Category N.**
   - Refer to Illustrations 1 (HV battery), 2 (Category S elements) and 3 (Category N elements); illustrations are generic — defer to manufacturer repair information.

## Definitions

- **Reusable part** — not contaminated, and whose future operational performance can be assessed and confirmed as not compromised, so warranty over correct future operation can be applied ("warranty" is not a guarantee by the salvage company/dismantler). Refer to manufacturer repair info for safety-related parts.
- **Non-reusable part** — future operational performance cannot be assessed/confirmed as uncompromised or uncontaminated, so no warranty. **Must NEVER be re-sold or re-used (non-exhaustive):** airbags; seat belts; seat-belt pre-tensioners; seat-belt stalks; seat-belt buckles and associated fixings. Also: refer to manufacturer info for other safety-critical components; inspect and only reuse if undamaged and warrantable; never reuse manufacturer-designated sacrificial components.
- **Recycling** — converting waste into reusable material / passing it through a system again for further treatment or use, to avoid unnecessary landfill/incineration.
- **Catastrophically damaged vehicle** — damage beyond safe repair to accepted industry standards without a bodyshell; or manufacturer/researched repair methods or service parts unavailable; or prior substandard repair / excessive corrosion.
- **Appropriately Qualified Person (AQP)** — comprehensive technical education and training in motor-vehicle repair, holding a current competency-based salvage-categorisation assessment from the IAEA (Institute of Automotive Engineer Assessors) or an equivalent recognised body. Determines and is responsible for the category given the peril (theft, collision, impact, fire, electrical, flood, contamination). Must be identifiable by a competency-based unique identifier.

Other terms: **MIAFTR** (Motor Insurance Anti-Fraud Theft Register); **CoD** (Certificate of Destruction, form **V860**); **V5C** (registered-keeper record); **VIN**; **ELV** (End of Life Vehicle); **ATF** (Authorised Treatment Facility); **HV** (High Voltage); **Monocoque** (body combined with chassis as a single unit); **SRS** (Supplementary Restraint System).

## Structural vs non-structural classification

**Structural (→ Category S if repairable; structural damage forces the CoP):** any element that, if damaged, requires repair, realignment to original dimensions, or replacement. From Illustration 2: firewall/front bulkhead; front & rear header rails; roof side cant rail; rear inner wing; rear wheel-housing extension; inner B-post/pillar reinforcement; inner sill reinforcement; front & rear chassis legs; front & rear 1-piece mega/giga castings; rear back-panel assembly; inner A-post/pillar reinforcement; front upper wing support; front inner wing wheelhouse; body structure for chassis frame; welded chassis frame; body side outer (minus quarter panel); HGV passenger cab.

**Non-structural (→ Category N if repairable):** outer-skin / bolt-on / cosmetic parts. From Illustration 3: front & rear bumpers; bonnet; front wing; front & rear bumper reinforcements/assembly; slam panel; door skin & door assembly; rear quarter panel; roof panel; boot floor; rear panel outer; boot lid / tailgate; load-bed assembly; apron filler panel. (Trim, glass and lights are likewise cosmetic/non-structural.)

**AQP notes:** assess intrusion of damage **behind** outer (non-structural) skin into structural components **before** assigning a category. The CoP definition is generic — always refer to manufacturer repair information.

## High-voltage (HV) EV battery rules (§8, applies to BEVs over 60 volts)

- A HV battery (>60 V) can form part of the structure. **Treat it as structural where the manufacturer's information shows the battery contributes to structural integrity** (e.g. battery acts as floor panel / structural crossmembers, or its removal compromises the floor — Illustration 1, Diagrams A & B). Where the floor has its own structural crossmembers and battery removal does not affect the structure, the battery is non-structural (Diagram C).
- **Structural HV battery damaged → Category S minimum** (repairable branch).
- **Non-structural HV battery damaged → may be Category N** — but only if the damaged HV battery is genuinely non-structural; HV systems may still be compromised.
- **Category A:** if the HV battery can be safely removed, remove and recycle it separately; if not (e.g. severe fire damage) recycle it as part of the complete vehicle.
- **Category B:** vehicle not repairable but functional parts reusable; HV batteries must be assessed for recycling or reuse (reuse only if warrantable).
- **Labelling/storage:** all HV vehicles or removed HV batteries must be stored in a controlled environment and safety-labelled for electric-shock danger.
- **Repairability/recycling:** some manufacturers deem batteries non-repairable (must be recycled); repairs only by a qualified HV technician. Flooded / partially fire-damaged batteries may still be recyclable (seek recycler advice); manufacturers are obligated to take back end-of-life HV batteries (see Appendix 1 / waste controls).

## Water / fire / smoke damage (§13)

**Water damage (§13.1):**
- **A** — no usable parts.
- **B** — reusable parts present; OR water ingress into the passenger compartment to a level compromising electrical/safety components (special care for battery/hybrid vehicles).
- **N** — all other water-damaged vehicles within the CoP.
- Buyers/part-purchasers must be told the nature and origin of the original damage; only safe usable parts may be resold.

**Fire / smoke / heat damage (§13.2):** first ask "does it contain reusable parts?"
- **A** — unrepairable, no reusable parts.
- **B** — unrepairable, with reusable parts.
- **S** — repairable, fire/heat damage to structural frame/chassis, owner chose not to repair.
- **N** — repairable, no fire/heat damage to structural frame/chassis, owner chose not to repair.
- Consider the effect of heat on structure and overall vehicle integrity.

**Motorcycles, derivatives & quadbikes/ATVs (§13.3):**
- **A** — not repairable, no usable parts.
- **B** — any one of: machine beyond safe repair; frame non-cosmetic damage where frame/chassis is alloy or composite; frame/engine VIN/identification tampered with; OR two-or-more of {fork assembly complete, power unit, swinging-arm assembly incl. suspension unit(s)} cannot be safely repaired. (Category B disposal = same as monocoque bodyshell/chassis/frame.)
- **S** — applies ONLY where frame/chassis is mild steel and can be safely repaired to manufacturer spec/method. **Category S NEVER applies to an alloy or composite frame/chassis.**
- **N** — none of the above; e.g. cosmetic (paint-only) frame damage on an alloy/composite frame.

## Body-structure disposal (§14)

**Monocoque (§14.1):** Category A and B body structures/chassis/frames must be **crushed in their entirety**. Only bolt-on panels and doors with welded (pinned) hinges may be removed if reusable. **No welded or bonded sections** may be salvaged. A **Certificate of Destruction (CoD)** must be issued. The structural framework **containing the VIN must be crushed**, and the VIN (visible and/or stamped) **must not be removed** from bodyshell/frame/chassis. Identification marks on engines and other reused parts must not be erased. Damaged structures replaced in service must be crushed (or returned to the manufacturer under an exchange scheme — no CoD to DVLA in that case, but insurer requires proof of destruction). Where a new manufacturer structure is unavailable and a replacement is needed for repair → **Category B**; never call a vehicle repairable on the basis that second-hand structures/elements/frames will be used (→ Category B).

**Separate body + chassis (§14.2):** for Category B, chassis frame and body shell are one component — both must be crushed and a notification of destruction produced for both. The chassis frame is the base structure and must be crushed in its entirety for all A and B cases. **Exception (commercial vehicle ≥7.5 t with separate cab):** the cab may be treated separately; for a Category B vehicle, a safely reusable cab can be sold — the dealer must remove any VIN plate and over-stamp any stamped-in VIN with crosses (original still legible); the purchaser then stamps the new chassis number adjacent. For Category S on such vehicles, a second-hand cab may be used as a replacement.

## Notification & records

- **MIAFTR entry is mandatory for every categorised vehicle**, showing the category, as soon as reasonably practical after inspection. Completing the MIAFTR entry meets the regulatory duty to notify DVLA under the Road Vehicles (Registration and Licensing) (Amendment) Regulations 2018. **All amended/updated information must be submitted within two working days of the final decision.** Do not delete MIAFTR records; retain entries for all previous incidents; correct only genuine errors. Any category change must be updated on MIAFTR (and notified to any party the vehicle was transferred to) as soon as practical after re-classification.
- **V5C / DVLA per category:**
  - **A** — *No V5C re-issued* by DVLA. V5C in your possession must be securely destroyed.
  - **B** — *No V5C re-issued*. V5C in your possession must be securely destroyed.
  - **S** — V5C will be **re-issued with a literal comment** depicting vehicle status.
  - **N** — V5C **re-issued, no literal comment** added.
  - The registered keeper must notify DVLA when the vehicle passes to an insurer/self-insurer on full & final settlement; the keeper may authorise the insurer to act as agent, but the insurer must notify DVLA via the appropriate V5C section without delay (else the keeper remains liable under Continuous Registration). All registration plates must be immediately covered/removed by the salvage agent.
- **Recovered Stolen Vehicles (RSV):** undamaged RSVs, or those with only minimal non-structural damage, fall **outside** the CoP, but **all recovered vehicles must still be updated on MIAFTR** and the record must not be deleted.
- **Certificate of Destruction (CoD = V860):** any vehicle designated end-of-life must be treated per current ELV Regulations and issued a CoD (V860). Any vehicle for which a CoD has been issued (regardless of insurance category) is **waste** and subject to waste controls.
- **Record retention:** CoD records retained by the salvage agent for **at least six years** (audit), or as defined in current regulations. (Hazardous-waste consignment records: 3 years — Appendix 1.) All salvage disposal documentation must include the salvage category; maintain an audit trail of purchases/disposals and record identities of vendors/purchasers (proof of ID for non-insurer parties).
- **Waste controls (Appendix 1):** apply to all ELVs — Category A, Category B, and any S/N vehicle to be broken for spares. ELVs are hazardous waste until fully depolluted; depollution only at an **ATF** (environmental permit). Export for dismantling/breaking → International Waste Shipment (IWS) controls apply (S/N exported for genuine reuse/repair are not waste exports).

## How to categorise — checklist

Gather inputs: peril (collision/theft/fire/flood/electrical/contamination), structural assessment (incl. intrusion behind outer skin), EV/HV-battery status (>60 V, structural per manufacturer?), water/fire/smoke flags, parts contamination, repair feasibility vs original/manufacturer methods. (Note: repair cost vs PAV/economic write-off is an insurer commercial decision; ABI category turns on **repairability + structural damage + reuse**, not on the cost ratio.)

1. **Q1 — In scope?** No/minimal damage and not structural → outside CoP. Any structural damage → continue.
2. **Q2 — Repairable?** Safely repairable to industry/manufacturer standards, original structural components available, not catastrophically damaged, not a theft-chain risk?
   - **No → Q3.** **Yes → Q4.**
   - No original structural parts / second-hand structure needed (and not a ≥7.5 t exception) → **Category B**.
3. **Q3 — Reusable parts?** (non-repairable)
   - None → **A** (crush whole vehicle after depollution + recyclable removal; no V5C; secure-destroy V5C; CoD/V860; HV battery removed & recycled or recycled with vehicle).
   - Yes → **B** (break for parts; crush VIN-bearing structure; no V5C; secure-destroy V5C; CoD; do not remove/erase VIN or engine ID; assess HV battery for recycle/reuse).
4. **Q4 — Structural damage (incl. structural HV battery)?** (repairable)
   - Yes → **S** (V5C re-issued with literal comment).
   - No → **N** (V5C re-issued, no literal comment; check residual safety-critical replacements).
5. **Apply overlays:** water (§13.1), fire/smoke (§13.2), motorcycle/quad (§13.3); structural HV battery forces S, non-structural HV battery permits N (§8); monocoque/separate-frame disposal rules (§14).
6. **Record & notify:** AQP records category + unique identifier and reasoning → **MIAFTR within 2 working days of final decision**; handle V5C/DVLA per category; issue CoD (V860) where ELV; update MIAFTR for any RSV or re-categorisation; retain records ≥6 years; apply ELV/waste/ATF/IWS controls for A, B, and S/N broken for spares.
